Using Generative AI

How I used tools like ChatGPT during this course — and why how you use them matters.

What Is Generative AI?

Generative AI refers to tools that can produce text, code, images, and other content based on a prompt you give them. The most well-known examples are ChatGPT by OpenAI, Google Gemini, and Claude by Anthropic. These tools have gotten very good very fast, and they came up a lot in conversations throughout this course — especially toward the end.

During CIS 130 I mostly used ChatGPT as a study aid, not as a way to do my assignments for me. There is a big difference between the two, and understanding that difference is what this page is really about.

How I Actually Used It

Getting Explanations When I Was Stuck

Sometimes after reading a lesson or watching a video, I still did not fully understand something. I would ask ChatGPT to explain it a different way — sometimes with a simpler analogy, sometimes with a more specific example. It was like having a tutor available at any hour. Flexbox and the CSS box model were two topics where this helped me a lot.

Debugging Code

When my code was not working and I could not figure out why, I would paste it into ChatGPT and describe the problem. Usually it could spot the issue quickly — a missing semicolon, an incorrect file path, a tag that was not closed. The important part was that I made myself understand the fix before moving on, not just paste it in and forget about it.

Checking My Understanding

I also used it to test myself. I would explain a concept back to ChatGPT in my own words and ask if my understanding was correct. This helped me catch gaps in my knowledge before assignments were due.

Key distinction: Using AI to understand something better is helpful. Using AI to produce work you then submit as your own without understanding it is academic dishonesty — and you will struggle on anything that builds on that topic later.

Using AI Responsibly

There are real risks to relying on AI too heavily, especially in a course where you are supposed to be building new skills. Here is what I kept in mind:

  • AI makes mistakes — It can give you code that looks correct but does not work, or explain something in a way that is subtly wrong. Always verify what it tells you.
  • It does not know your assignment — AI gives general answers. It does not know your professor's specific requirements or the context of your class.
  • Over-reliance creates gaps — If AI writes all your code, you will not be able to debug it yourself or build on it in future courses.
  • Follow your school's policy — Academic honesty rules apply to AI-generated content. When in doubt, ask your professor what is allowed.
  • Protect your privacy — Do not share personal information, passwords, or private data in any AI chat interface.
