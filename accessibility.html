Accessibility

Building websites that everyone can use — regardless of ability or disability.

What Is Web Accessibility?

Before Week 7, I honestly had not thought much about who could or could not use a website. I just assumed if it looked fine on my screen, it was fine. This week completely changed how I think about building for the web.

Web accessibility means making sure your site works for people with visual, auditory, motor, or cognitive disabilities. That includes people who use screen readers, people who navigate with only a keyboard, and people with color vision differences. It is not a bonus feature — it is part of building a website correctly. In many cases it is also a legal requirement.

What I Applied to My Own Pages

Alt Text on Every Image

Every <img> tag needs an alt attribute. Screen readers read that text aloud to describe the image to someone who cannot see it. If the image is just decorative, you still include the attribute but leave it empty: alt="". This tells screen readers to skip it rather than read out a confusing filename.

Proper Heading Order

Headings need to go in logical order — <h1> first, then <h2>, then <h3>, and so on. You should never skip a level. Screen reader users often jump between headings to navigate a page, so if your heading structure is broken, the page becomes very hard to get around.

Color Contrast

Text needs to be readable against its background. The standard is a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 for regular text. I used the WebAIM Contrast Checker to test all my color combinations before finalizing them. Some colors that look fine to me might be nearly invisible to someone with low vision or color blindness.

Keyboard Navigation

Not everyone uses a mouse. I added a "Skip to main content" link at the very top of each page — it is invisible unless you tab to it, but keyboard users can use it to jump past the navigation and get straight to the content. This is one of those small details that makes a big difference for certain users.

Descriptive Link Text

Links should describe where they go. "Click here" or "read more" means nothing to a screen reader user who is tabbing through links on a page. I made sure all my links use text that makes sense on its own.

Quick check: Tab through your page using only your keyboard and see if you can reach everything. If you cannot, neither can your keyboard-only users.

Tools We Used to Test Accessibility

  • WebAIM Contrast Checker — Tests your text and background color combination and tells you if it passes WCAG standards. Open Contrast Checker
  • WAVE Accessibility Tool — Analyzes a live webpage and highlights accessibility errors visually right on the page. Really useful for seeing exactly where the problems are. Open WAVE Tool
  • Chrome Lighthouse — Built into Chrome's developer tools. Gives your page an accessibility score and lists specific things to fix.

Helpful Article

The WebAIM Introduction to Web Accessibility was one of the assigned readings for Week 7 and it is genuinely one of the best overviews I came across. It explains why accessibility matters, who it affects, and the principles behind it without being overly technical. Worth reading even if it is not required for your class.
