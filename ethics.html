Ethical Issues in Web Development

Using media responsibly, respecting copyright, and being honest with your users.

Why Ethics Matter on the Web

Week 6 introduced something I had not really thought about before — the ethical side of building websites. It is easy to focus on whether your code works and forget that the decisions you make as a developer actually affect real people. What images you use, how you collect data, and how you design your interface all have consequences.

This was one of the more eye-opening weeks in the course for me, especially the section on copyright. I had definitely used images from Google before without thinking twice about it.

Copyright and Images

Just because an image shows up in a Google search does not mean you can use it. Almost everything online is automatically protected by copyright the moment it is created. Using someone else's image without permission — even on a small personal website — is technically copyright infringement.

Where to Find Images You Can Actually Use

In this course we used Pixabay, which is a site that offers free images released under a license that allows you to use them for almost any purpose, including web projects, without needing to give credit (though it is still good practice to do so).

Other reliable sources for free, legally usable images include:

  • Unsplash — High-quality free photos contributed by photographers
  • Pexels — Free stock photos and videos with a generous license
  • Creative Commons Search — Lets you search openly licensed content across many platforms

Giving Credit Even When You Don't Have To

Some licenses do not require you to credit the creator, but doing it anyway is just good practice. It respects the person who made the work and it models the kind of honesty online that makes the internet a better place.

Simple rule: If you did not make it and you are not sure you are allowed to use it — do not use it. Always check the license before adding any image, video, or audio to your site.

Other Ethical Responsibilities

User Privacy and Data

If your website collects any information from users — even just an email address through a form — you have a responsibility to protect that data and be transparent about how you use it. Users should always know what they are signing up for.

Dark Patterns

Dark patterns are design choices that manipulate users into doing things they did not intend — like making the "unsubscribe" button tiny and hard to find, or pre-checking boxes for services people did not ask for. Ethical developers design interfaces that are clear and respect the user's choices.

Honesty and Accuracy

Publishing misleading or false information on a website is an ethical issue too. As a developer and content creator, you are responsible for what you put online. Cite your sources and verify information before publishing it.
